Meet Me

I am a PhD candidate in Mayra Vidal's lab at the University of Massachusetts, Boston. I use laboratory and genomic studies to better understand the ecological and evolutionary dynamics of dietary generalism in insect herbivores. I am a NSF Graduate Research Fellow and an alum of Georgetown University, where I worked with Gina Wimp and Matt Hamilton on the ecological genetics of salt marshes. You can view my CV here. Research

At UMass, with our collaborators at Georgetown, Smith, and University of Denver, we use fall webworm (Hyphantria cunea) to understand the evolution of dietary generalism. Fall webworm exhibits variation in host plant use and diet breadth across its wide geographic range and between its two morphotypes, making it a tractable system to study how genetic and ecological variation among morphotypes and localities can influence host plant use and diet breadth. We hope to gain insight into how ecological and evolutionary processes interact to produce patterns of host use and diversification in insect herbivores.


Tradeoffs that govern diet breadth are hard to detect and may emerge at large scales, so we use large reciprocal transplant experiments, sometimes rearing over 10,000 larvae, to test for local adaptation and tradeoffs across hosts in generalists.


Host use tradeoffs might be resolved in insect herbivores through divergent selection and local adaptation or through plasticity. I use whole genome resequencing studies of wild captured individuals to study the genomic basis of local adaptation and whole methylome sequencing of experimental individuals to study the epigenetic basis of plasticity. To do this work, we needed a reference genome for fall webworm, so we produced one and published it in Journal of Heredity last year. I am also interested in whether the evolution of generalism evolves in parallel between the two morphotypes of fall webworm, and have work exploring this question coming soon.

At Georgetown, I used microsattlite markers to study the population effects of sea level rise induced habitat loss on Tumidagena minuta. We found that despite radpid and precipitous declines in habitat area and connectivity, T. minuta populations reamained large and resisted genetic differentiation and allele loss through drift.
