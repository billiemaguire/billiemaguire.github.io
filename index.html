Meet Me

I am a PhD candiate in Mayra Vidal's lab at the University of Massachusetts, Boston. I use laboratory and genomic studies to better understand the ecological and evolutionary dynamics of dietary generalism in insect herbivores. I am a NSF Graduate Research Fellow and an alum of Georgetown University, where I worked with Gina Wimp and Matt Hamilton on the ecological genetics of salt marshes. You can view my CV here. Research

At UMass, we use fall webworm (Hyphantria cunea) to understand the evolution of dietary generalsim. Fall webworm exhibits vartiation in host plant use and diet breadth across its wide geographic range and between its two morphotypes, making it a tractable system to study how genetic and ecological variation among morphotypes and localities can influence host plant use and diet breadth. We hope to gain insight into how ecological and evolutionary processes interact to produce patterns of host use and diversification in insect herbivores.


Tradeoffs that govern diet breath are hard to detect and may emerge at large scales, so we use large reciprocal transplant experiments, sometimes rer rearing over 10,000 larvae, to test to local adaptation and host use preferences


At Georgetown, I used microsattlite markers to study the population effects of sea level rise induced habitat loss on Tumidagena minuta. We found that despite radpid and precipitous declines in habitat area and connectivity, T. minuta populations reamained large and resisted genetic differentiation and allele loss through drift.


Education and Outreach

I strive to be a mentor for others in the lab and those in my classes by connecting them to the resources and and oppurtunities that helped me find my way and by cultivating a lively and friendly community of scholars.


University of Massachusetts, Boston is the third most diverse four-year institution in the country. This means that our mentorhsip and outreach are especially impactful for diversity and inclusion in ecology and that we have a indespensible oppurtunity to make our research better and our community stronger by creating oppurtunites for our students. I am looking foward to doing my part to build a community around a shared interest in ecology, evolutionary biology, natural history, and enviromentalism.


UMass is located in Dorchester, a diverse working class neighboorhood in south Boston. We are excited connect our research to this community by bringing the Caterpillar Roadshow to youth spaces in Dorchester. I'm also working on some "community taxonomy" projects and to have some online content soon!
